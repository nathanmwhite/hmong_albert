administrative officers and boads.
the governor does not control all the administrative functions of the
state. some administrative positions are filled by elective officials
who are completely independent of him. chief among them are the
secretary of state, who countersigns official documents, issues
certificates of incorporation to newly charted corporations, and keeps
the official records; the state treasure, who is the custodian of
state funds; the state resul auditor, who certifies the legality of
monies paid into and out of the state treasury; the attorney general,
who conducts the legal affairs of the state, and the superintendent of
public instruction, who is in charge of education.
then there are the state boards. they also tend to function
independently of the governor. they are created by action of the
legislature which often feels that certain areas of administration
ought to be nonpolitical and handled by nonpartisan boards. the seldom
works well in practice, since the board members-usually three to seven
appointees-end up by trying to aid both major parties. the tittles and
functions of these various boards differ greatly from state to state.
but in each state there are rule-making boards, licensing boards, and
straight administrative boards. most states have more them 50 such
state departments. they administer a great many important activities
including public works, health and welfare, social security, penal
institutions, public utilities, and so on. naturally, this dispersal
of control among the governor, other administrative officials, and the
state boards causes duplication of effort. it often results in
inefficiency and waste. good administration, on the other hand, calls
for the organization of executive functions under the control of one
responsible head.
