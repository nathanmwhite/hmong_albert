2.  tb screening
some questions have been raised regarding the scheduling of tb
screening for the initial group of cases cancelled from flights from 23
january – 15 february. originally, when the january flights were
cancelled, it was hoped that the february flights would go ahead.
therefore, the medical unit began chest x-rays and skin tests the
groups scheduled to depart in february. later, the february flights
were also cancelled and those cases cancelled from january flights were
called in for screening.
in addition to travel ready cases, those cases which have immediate
family members diagnosed with tb will also be scheduled for chest
x-rays.  this will assist the doctors in determining if the tb has
spread from one family member to another.
for cases that are hard cross-referenced to another case in which
someone is undergoing tb treatment, chest x-rays will be scheduled once
the person with tb completes treatment (i.e., six months after
beginning tb medication). this will help ensure that hard
cross-referenced cases do not become travel ready at different times.
the tb testing and physical exams for individuals originally booked to
depart in january and february will be completed within the next 3 to 4
weeks.  once this group has been completed, a new group will be
scheduled for x-rays, skin tests and physical exams.  the scheduling of
the new group will be based on when a case became travel ready (for
example, those who became travel ready in november 2004 will be
scheduled before those who became travel ready in january 2005).
we believe that the results of the vast majority of sputum cultures
will come back negative after 8 weeks and large numbers will then be
able to travel. iom has a lot of experience with tb treatment in south
east asia. based on this experience, we anticipate a 100% success rate
for those diagnosed with regular tb following 6 months of treatment.
for the very small number of cases with multi-drug resistant tb, the
treatment will take up to two years.

3.  sputum collection
we understand that people are upset about having to undergo new
procedures such as sputum collection; however, we would again like to
emphasize that it is imperative that individuals comply with all
procedures as requested by the medical unit.
this week only 75% scheduled for sputum collection showed up. for the
children, the no-show rate was 50%.  we understand that small children
may be frightened by having a rubber tube inserted into their stomachs;
however, we have found that when parents help their children to cough
most of the children can produce sufficient sputum in wtk and do not
need to go to the hospital.  in order to avoid further delays, we
encourage all those scheduled to show up for sputum collection.  sputum
samples must be given for three consecutive days.

4.  self medicating
rumors are circulating that some of the hmong are buying medication
outside wtk in an effort to test negative for tb. this is a very
dangerous thing to do.  not only will the medication not change the
test results, taking outside medication can lead to the development of
drug resistant strains of tb and make treatment more difficult.  we
encourage you to comply with the instructions and procedures of the iom
medical unit. this in the best interest of your health and timely
departure to the us.
